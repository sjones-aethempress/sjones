When you live in a multi-tenant building, you learn people's habits without ever meaning to. You know who clatters down the hall with grocery bags every Tuesday night, who slams their door hard enough to make the frame rattle, and who insists on wearing cologne so strong you can smell them before you see them.

But my favorite was Ms. Everly.

She was in her seventies or eighties, a tiny woman with short, curled hair the exact shade of dryer lint, and horn-rimmed glasses that always seemed a little crooked. She carried herself with a matronly air, the sort of person who could have scolded you into behaving without raising her voice. The way she smiled made you feel like you'd just passed some invisible test. She reminded me of Sophia from _The Golden Girls_, right down to her sly, knowing look.

Her right arm didn't move quite like it should, so when she waved, she kept her elbow tucked in close to her ribs, her forearm lifting in a half-salute. It was a distinctive motion, one you didn't forget after seeing it once.

She lived in a unit at the very end of one hallway. I lived at the far end of the opposite wing. Sometimes our paths crossed when I was heading to the elevator, and every so often I'd help her carry her groceries inside. She always thanked me like I'd just performed a heroic rescue, even if all I'd done was keep her from dropping a loaf of bread.

One evening, I stepped out of the elevator with a stack of mail under my arm and saw her. She was standing outside her apartment, smiling warmly, and gave me her signature tucked-elbow wave.

"Evening, Ms. Everly," I called down the hallway.

She didn't answer, but her smile didn't fade. She turned, opened her door, and slipped inside.

I thought maybe she hadn't heard me, so I walked the length of the hallway to her apartment and knocked. No answer. I tried again. Still nothing.

I chalked it up to her having gone straight to bed and headed back to my place.

The next morning, I stopped at the concierge desk to pick up a package. Making small talk, I mentioned seeing Ms. Everly.

"Is she doing okay?" I asked.

The concierge's expression shifted. "You didn’t hear?"

"Hear what?"

"Ms. Everly died last week."

I stared at him, certain I'd misunderstood.

"No," I said slowly. "I saw her last night."

He shook his head. "Her family came and cleared her place out days ago. Nobody's been in there since."

I left the desk with my package, but my thoughts were stuck on her smile, her wave, but not the sound of her apartment door closing. My brain tried to make it fit into something logical. Maybe someone else had been visiting her place, maybe I'd just seen them from too far away to tell. But no. It had been her.

The days passed. Life went on. I stopped myself from mentioning it to other tenants. Nobody wants to be the one in the building telling ghost stories like a bad camp counselor.

It would have stayed that way, too, except for what happened a year later.

I was down in the laundry room talking with a newer tenant. We were swapping "weird things about this building" stories, like the dryer that needed to be kicked twice before starting, the elevator that sometimes stopped at the wrong floor.

"You ever see the old lady?" the tenant asked casually.

Something cold crept into my spine. "What old lady?" I asked.

"She's at the end of the hallway sometimes. Smiles and waves, then walks into a door without opening it. Happened to me twice."

Before I could respond, another tenant passing through spoke up. "Oh yeah, I've seen her too. She disappears if you get close. Like, gone. Just not there anymore."

I must have looked like I was trying not to breathe.

They both went on talking about her as if she were just another quirk of the building, like the finicky elevator. But every detail matched exactly, the wave, the smile, the quiet presence, right down to where she appeared.

Since then, I've caught myself glancing down that hallway more often than I'd like to admit. I don't linger. I don't go looking. But sometimes I wonder if she's there, just past the point where the light fixtures start to hum.

Part of me would like to see her again, to wave back, maybe even follow her through that door.

But another part of me wonders if I ever got that close, I might not come back.
