I had never seen my dad so proud as the day he came home between business trips, parked his company-issued Ford Thunderbird in the driveway, and opened the back door with a smile. Out bounded an Irish Setter, all gangly legs and bright eyes. The puppy seemed almost too big for his own body, his paws clumsy on the grass, his tail wagging like a metronome set to "pure joy."

That enthusiasm caught me right away. The pup's name was Ralph, and he was maybe two or three months old. His fur was the color of autumn leaves just before they fall, and his ears flopped in a way that made you forgive him for every future sock he would chew.

Dad didn't get to stay long. Another business trip had him back on the road almost immediately, but Ralph and I had the run of the yard that first day. We played until the sun dipped low and the lightning bugs started blinking in the warm Georgia air. He followed me everywhere, his nose twitching at every scent, his eyes trusting me as if I had all the answers.

But the second day, something changed. Ralph seemed tired. His eyes dulled, and he didn't want to chase his ball.

I thought he was just worn out from the excitement. By the third day, though, my worry turned to confusion and fear. His breathing was shallow, and he wouldn't touch his food. That's when Mom and I loaded him into the car and drove to see Dr. Ellinberg, the vet who had cared for every animal we'd ever had.

Dr. Ellinberg examined Ralph quietly. Then he sat me down in that small, linoleum-floored office and explained something I was too young to fully understand but old enough to feel in my bones. The people who had given Ralph to my dad had known he was sick with Parvo. They had known, and they had said nothing.

It was an ugly truth to swallow, but there was no time to dwell on anger. The doctor told me that Ralph was in pain, that he was not going to get better, and that I needed to make a decision. It was my first time being given the kind of choice adults make, the kind that comes with a weight you never really put down.

I kissed Ralph goodbye, my face buried in that warm red fur. His body was still small enough to fit in my arms, still trusting me even as his strength faded. The vet gave me a cardboard box for him, and I placed a Gideon Bible inside because I had seen enough funerals that year to know you didn't send someone on their way without some words for the road.

We had buried no fewer than a dozen family members in 1981. Mom and Dad's aunts, uncles, cousins... grief had become its own kind of unwanted houseguest that year. Now, at the edge of our yard, under the peach tree that stood between us and the neighbor's property, I dug another small grave. Mrs. Edwards, my adopted third grandmother, stood on her porch and watched quietly. When I finished, she walked over, patted my shoulder, and said nothing, which was the right thing to do.

Dad came home from his trip a few days later, expecting to see Ralph bounding toward him. Instead, he found me quiet and the yard still. I told him what had happened, and I saw his face fall in a way I had never seen before. He didn't say much, just a simple, "I'm sorry, son," but the way his voice caught and his shoulders dropped told me everything. He felt like he had let me down, though I never saw it that way.

Ralph hadn't been with us long, but the short time we had mattered. He was a gift, even if his life was a brief one. I think my Dad knew that. Maybe that was why, over the years, he made a point to bring home little surprises when he could. It wasn't about replacing Ralph. It was about never letting me forget that joy can walk through your back door at any moment.
