There is no greater test of human decency, patience, or pure social awkwardness than deciding whether to hold the door for someone who is just a hair too far away. You see them. They see you. In that instant, both of you know you are trapped in a game of chicken played at the speed of a snail, and nobody is going to win.

In the South, door-holding is practically sacred. Not holding the door is the social equivalent of skipping church on Easter. It is not just rude, it is unthinkable. This little dance of courtesy, however, is also one of the most stressful rituals known to mankind. Because once you commit, there is no going back.

The first question is always: how far is too far? Ten feet is safe. Fifteen feet is iffy. Once you hit twenty, you are basically waving them into next week. The math changes depending on whether the person is old, young, or carrying a toddler and a casserole dish. I once held the door for a man who was so far away that I had time to rethink my entire career choices before he got there. By the time he finally shuffled through, I was convinced I had aged a decade.

Timing is everything. If you hold it too soon, the other person feels obligated to jog, which creates its own set of problems. Too late, and you look like the kind of person who would cut in line at a funeral procession. It is the social equivalent of trying to defuse a bomb. One wrong move and the whole thing blows up in a cloud of awkwardness.

Of course, if you are the person walking toward the door, you immediately feel the pressure to pick up the pace. There is nothing dignified about it. Nobody breaks into a confident Olympic stride. It is always that weird half-run, half-waddle that makes you look like a penguin running late for a job interview.

I cannot tell you how many times I have done this ridiculous shuffle, mumbling "almost there" like I'm approaching the finish line of a marathon. Meanwhile, the person holding the door is silently regretting every choice that led them to this moment. Both parties know this is absurd, but you cannot quit halfway. You are in it together, locked in a contract of shared embarrassment.

Then there are the people who take door-holding to an extreme. Some folks treat it like their personal calling. They will stand there for minutes, smiling politely, while an entire crowd marches through as if they are the hotel doorman. I once saw a man hold the door for what must have been an entire family reunion. Cousins, great-aunts, toddlers, and even a family dog went through. By the time the last person passed, the poor man's arm was stuck at a ninety-degree angle, frozen like a scarecrow guarding the fellowship hall.

And yet, in the South, this is seen as noble. Door-holding is not just polite, it is practically a moral obligation. Where I come from, you are raised to believe that the Lord is keeping score. Up North, you are lucky if someone does not slam the door directly in your face. Down here, if you let one close too quickly, you will never hear the end of it at Sunday lunch.

Nothing stings worse than holding a door for someone who does not even say thank you. You stand there, beaming goodwill into the world, and they breeze through like you were hired to do it. I do not expect a brass band or a standing ovation, but a nod of the head or a muttered "thanks" would not kill anyone.

In the South, we deal with this in the most polite way possible: the bless-your-heart glare. It is the kind of smile that looks sweet on the surface but could peel paint if it lingers too long. We would never call you rude to your face, but we will absolutely bring it up when retelling the story later. And make no mistake, it will be retold.

Sometimes, though, you just have to admit defeat. If they're more than thirty feet away, it is not rude to let the door swing shut. At that point, you are not being impolite; you are just obeying the laws of physics. There is only so long you can hold a slab of glass and metal before it starts to feel less like kindness and more like unpaid labor.

Still, letting go is hard. I was trained from birth to treat door-holding as the cornerstone of civilization. It feels wrong to let it close, even when logic says otherwise. But in the end, the whole thing is not about perfection. It is about kindness. If it ends in awkwardness, well, at least you tried.

Door-holding is one of those social rituals that proves how complicated humans can make even the simplest tasks. What should be a small courtesy often spirals into a ballet of penguin jogs, frozen elbows, and bless-your-heart glares. But there is also something beautiful about it. It is a tiny reminder that we are all in this together, fumbling through life one awkward gesture at a time.

So the next time you find yourself holding the door for someone who is just a smidge too far away, remember this: you are not just being polite. You are participating in a grand tradition of Southern hospitality, awkward penguin jogs included.
