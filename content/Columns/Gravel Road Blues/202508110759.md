I have learned as I've gotten older that things just get bigger and flashier in America, and Texas doubly so.

When I was a youth, we had a single family grocery store smaller than most drug stores called Parsons. You could buy glass-bottle sodas for a dime and even get change back when you returned the bottles. A quarter could get you a Coke, a MoonPie, and the kind of sugar high that fueled the entire afternoon's adventures.

About a hundred yards down the street was a small cluster of buildings — a NAPA auto parts store, a barber shop, and a dry cleaner. Back then, "downtown" was often just three businesses and a bench, and the bench was probably where the mayor sat.

My parents each had their own errands in this little kingdom. Mom's world was the dry cleaner — a quiet place that smelled like starch and responsibility, with plastic-shrouded shirts swishing on a conveyor rack. The lady behind the counter wore cat-eye glasses on a chain and could count back change without looking. Going there with Mom meant standing still and trying not to bump into anything, because everything felt fragile and formal.

Dad's domain was the NAPA store. That was an entirely different energy. The minute you walked in, a little bell on the door announced you like you were somebody, and every man in the place looked up from behind the counter or the open hood of a car to nod and call out, "Hey, Earl!" or "Morning, buddy!" even if your name was neither Earl nor Buddy. It was a place where parts had names you couldn't spell but were somehow "in stock in the back." You could hear the satisfying thunk of a box landing on the counter and the snap of a cash register that might've been older than the state of Georgia.

And right between those two worlds — the pressed shirt world and the grease-under-the-nails world — sat the barber shop.

If the NAPA store was about fixing things and the dry cleaner about keeping them looking new, the barber shop was where you accepted you were getting older but tried to look sharp doing it. Mine had two chairs, a jar of blue disinfectant with combs standing like picket fences, and the scent of talcum powder that would cling to you for hours.

The walls were lined with framed Norman Rockwell prints — the kind where every person's expression told a whole story. There was the boy getting his hair cut for the first time, looking somewhere between proud and terrified. The small-town doctor was listening to a kid's heartbeat. Every print was like a mirror for the place itself: a reminder that these four walls held the same rituals and rhythms Rockwell painted decades earlier.

While Dad got his haircut, I'd sit in the cracked leather waiting chair, flipping through Field & Stream or a months-old Sports Illustrated. Every so often, one of the old-timers would look over the top of his newspaper and ask if I was "helping Dad out at the shop yet," which in my case meant carrying a box or holding a flashlight while he muttered about bolts that wouldn't come loose.

These three places — NAPA, barber, dry cleaner — were the orbit of my childhood errands. And each had its own unspoken etiquette. At the dry cleaner, you stood still. At NAPA, you nodded like you understood carburetors. At the barber, you sat quietly unless the barber asked you a question, in which case you'd better answer clearly because the whole shop was listening.

It all felt smaller back then — not just the buildings, but the spaces between people. We knew the folks who handled our clothes, cut our hair, and found the right alternator belt. They knew us back. Nowadays, the clerk at the big-box store doesn't know your name, and the barber's shop is just another franchise. Norman Rockwell's people have been replaced with stock photography. And you can order a carburetor online without so much as a nod from Earl.

But in the 1970s, the day itself was the content. You didn't have to record it or post it; you just lived it. You smelled the starch at the dry cleaner, heard the laughter at NAPA, and sat under the gaze of those Rockwell prints while the barber's scissors worked away. It was enough.
