If you grew up in the South, you know the power of the church bulletin. It is not just folded paper with hymn numbers printed inside. It is holy writ mixed with small-town gossip, a weekly declaration of who is on the potluck committee, and who forgot to turn in their casserole for the funeral supper. The bulletin is both scripture and scandal, handed out with a smile by an usher in a suit two sizes too big. It is the program, the plan, and sometimes the punchline.

The creation of a bulletin is never simple. The pastor has a vision, the church secretary has the typewriter or computer, and then there is always that one congregant who insists on proofreading every comma. Fonts are argued over as if eternal salvation depended on whether you choose Times New Roman or Arial. Placement is another battlefield. Should the revival notice go on the front, or does Sister Myrtle's pie auction deserve top billing this week? More than one fellowship hall feud has started over bold type.

And then there are the mistakes. Nothing creates confusion faster than a misprint in the Word of God's weekly newsletter. One time, instead of "Baptism Sunday," the bulletin proudly proclaimed "Baptism Sundae," which filled the children with hope and the adults with dread. Another time, the schedule read, "Choir practice canceled due to sin." That was supposed to be "snow." It was February. For three weeks, people speculated about who in the alto section had backslid.

The hierarchy of announcements is clear, though no one will admit it out loud. Big events like tent revivals, fish fries, and youth car washes are front and center. Bible study and Wednesday night prayer meeting are tucked in the back like the fine print on an insurance policy. Babies always make the cut, though sometimes you notice one family's joy highlighted in bold while another newborn barely makes the bottom line. If your name appears in italics, it might as well be engraved in stone. If it is left out entirely, expect side-eye glances over Sunday lunch.

Lunch, of course, is where the bulletin gets its second life. Families gather around fried chicken and mashed potatoes and dissect the announcements like archaeologists. Did you see Myrtle's fundraiser got page two instead of the cover? Did you catch that typo in the choir schedule? Was it intentional that the bake sale and the Weight Watchers meeting were scheduled for the same night? By dessert, entire reputations have risen and fallen based on a single line of bold print.

But the real magic is in the bloopers. Every church has them. "Don't let worry kill you—let the church help." That gem nearly caused the organist to fall off the bench. "The peace-making meeting has been canceled due to a conflict." You cannot script comedy better than that. Or my personal favorite: "The choir invites any member of the congregation who enjoys sinning to join." Membership doubled that week, though attendance fell off when everyone realized the sinning part was just a typo.

Then there are the more unfortunate phrasing choices, like "Ladies' Bible Study will meet Thursday morning. All ladies are invited to lunch in the Fellowship Hall after the BS is done." Or "Please place your donation in the envelope along with the deceased person you want remembered." Nothing says devotion quite like stuffing Grandma into an offering plate.

And who could forget the sermon announcements? "This morning: Jesus Walks on Water. Tonight: Searching for Jesus." It leaves you wondering what happened in between. Or the Sunday when the pastor was preaching his farewell message, followed by the choir singing "Break Forth Into Joy." Subtlety has never been the strong suit of church bulletins.

For all their quirks, the bulletin still matters. It is a touchstone, a tangible piece of tradition in a world where everything else has gone digital. You can put the announcements on a website, but nobody is going to fold up a web page and tuck it into their Bible next to their grandmother's funeral program. The bulletin is part of the rhythm of Sunday. It tells you who we are, what we value, and sometimes, unintentionally, what we find hilarious.

I will admit, some of my fondest church memories come not from the sermons but from stifling laughter while reading the morning announcements. A bulletin misprint can bring an entire congregation to tears of joy, and there is something comforting about knowing you are all in on the joke together.

The church bulletin may not always be gospel truth, but it is certainly the Bible of every congregation. Whether it tells you where to sit, what to sing, or accidentally suggests the ushers will eat latecomers, it holds a place of honor. And come Sunday lunch, you can bet it will be the most read piece of literature in town.
