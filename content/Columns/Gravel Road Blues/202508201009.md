Lindy lived across the street. His dad was the postmaster, which gave him an air of importance he did not deserve. Lindy was younger than me but bigger, and because of this, the older neighborhood kids liked to egg him on to hassle me. They saw him as a kind of blunt instrument, not much finesse, but solid enough to cause trouble.

Most of the time, I could dodge him or talk my way out of it. But one sunny afternoon, he picked exactly the wrong moment to start his routine.

I had been knocking golf balls around in the yard. My dad had given me a putter, the kind of hand-me-down treasure that came with rules. His exact words had been, “Take care of it, or it’ll be your butt.” I believed him. I treated that club with reverence, wiping it down after each use, like I was polishing a family heirloom instead of a cheap old putter.

Lindy wandered over, probably bored, probably looking for someone to push around. The moment he spotted me, he started in with his usual taunts. I tried to ignore him, but he kept getting closer, circling, smirking, and generally acting like the world’s largest mosquito.

He reached out and shoved me, not hard, but enough to rattle me. I told him to knock it off. He did not.

Somewhere in the tangled wires of my child brain, the message was clear: defend yourself. And in my hand was the perfect weapon.

I do not remember deciding to do it. One moment, Lindy was in front of me, still mouthing off. Next, the putter was in motion. I brought it down right on top of his head.

Now, to be clear, I did not hit him hard enough to cause serious injury. But the sound it made — a metallic thunk that could have been a sound effect in a cartoon — told me two things:

1. Lindy was going to stop hassling me for at least a little while.
    
2. This putter’s career as a golf club had just ended.
    

When I lifted it to inspect the damage, my stomach dropped. The head was bent at a perfect 90-degree angle, like a capital L. It was no longer a sporting tool. It was modern art.

Lindy, rubbing his head, decided this was a good time to head home. I stood there holding the evidence, knowing there was no hiding it. My dad’s voice echoed in my head: “It’ll be your ass.”

I walked home with the club in hand, feeling like I was marching to the gallows.

My mom was in the kitchen. She took one look at the putter and one look at my face, and her eyebrows went up. “Oh, your daddy is going to have your butt for this,” she said.

I nodded, resigned to my fate.

A while later, my dad came home from work. He took his time settling in before asking, “So what happened to the putter?”

I told him the truth. I explained about Lindy, the pushing, and how I had defended myself. I held the club up for him to see the new, avant-garde shape it had taken.

He nodded once, not saying much, then walked out of the room. I sat there, waiting for the inevitable lecture or grounding. Instead, I heard something from the bedroom, a muffled sound that took me a second to place.

It was laughter.

Not just a chuckle. The kind of deep, shoulder-shaking laugh you try to hold back but cannot. He was in there trying to keep me from hearing it, which only made it funnier.

I never did get another golf club. My short-lived golfing career ended right there. But I also never got punished for what happened, which felt like some kind of win.

Later that evening, I overheard my dad talking to my mom. “I’m just glad they weren’t playing baseball,” he said.

That was the thing about my dad. He had a way of cutting through a situation to the part that really mattered. Bent putter? Replaceable. Me? Also replaceable, but hopefully not anytime soon.

And as for Lindy, he kept his distance after that. Sometimes you only need to send one message, and sometimes that message comes in the shape of a capital L.
