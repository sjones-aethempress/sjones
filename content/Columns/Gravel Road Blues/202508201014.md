I used to run around with my cousins, who were all farriers and nationally-ranked calf ropers, which meant I got a crash course in rodeos, pickup trucks, and late-night Waffle Houses. There were two Kevins in our circle, which could have been confusing, but we solved it by calling them KB and KK. KB was my cousin, and KK was a friend of mine. He and I clicked right away because neither of us had a wide circle of buddies at the time. He was awkward, with a raw sense of humor, and hungry for companionship. I was about the same, which made us a natural pair.

One night, after a rodeo, we stopped at a Waffle House for dinner. Nothing says "long day’s work" like a greasy booth, coffee that tastes like the pot has been on since Nixon, and hash browns scattered and covered. A short, cheerful red-haired waitress came over and took our order with the easy confidence of someone who knew how to keep rodeo boys in line. She laughed at our dumb jokes and topped up the coffee without us asking.

That's when KK saw her. Not our waitress, but the other one, the blonde. She was tall, almost statuesque, the kind of woman who looked like she belonged in Milan instead of Maysville. Even under the hum of fluorescent lights, she stood out. KK, who was newly divorced and reeling from the loneliness of it, went into full-on girl-crazy mode.

We teased him because that is the sacred duty of friends, but then he decided to double down. He bet my cousin that before we left, he'd have her phone number written on a napkin. That was bold talk, but KK was nothing if not determined.

When our waitress came back for a refill, he leaned in and whispered something to her. She grinned like a cat who had just found a birdcage door left open. As she strolled past the blonde, she paused, said something low, motioned to the coffee pot, and then pointed back at us. The blonde smiled. KK puffed up like he had just roped a steer in record time.

Here's the problem, though. I was sitting at an angle where I caught something KK didn't. Something important. And once I saw it, I couldn't unsee it. A funny little classified ad my mentor had once written came to mind, and I nearly lost it right there in the booth. KK noticed.

"You don’t think I can get her number, do you?" he said.

"Oh, I have full faith in you," I managed to reply, trying not to spit out my coffee.

She finally came over, gliding like she had practiced the move. She poured our cups with a steady hand, leaned in as KK turned on the charm, and after a bit of back and forth, she scribbled her name and number on the corner of a napkin. She slid it across the table with a smile, and KK, feeling like a conquering hero, gave her another syrupy compliment.

And then it happened. She couldn't help it. His words tickled her just enough that she broke out in a full, open grin.

KK froze.

The girl was beautiful, sure. But her smile revealed something he hadn't accounted for. She had three teeth. Not three missing teeth. Three total teeth, spaced out like they were social distancing.

I nearly choked. KK blinked, still processing the revelation, while my cousins forced polite smiles and held themselves steady, doing everything they could not to laugh in her presence. To their credit, they never did. The ride home, however, was another story entirely.

Every bump in the road was another jab. "Hey, KK, maybe she's just saving on dental floss." "Bet she gets a discount on toothpaste." The jokes kept coming. He slumped down in the seat like a man on death row.

Finally, he groaned, "Why me?"

I told him, "Look, you owe her at least one date. She was sweet, kind, and honestly, good-hearted. Teeth are fixable. You can't fix crazy."

It was the best advice I had at the time, and maybe the truest.

The ad from Cat's Cradle? "_Gentleman farmer, 61, seeks toothless woman for quiet country life. Object: matrimony._"

KK never called her, at least not that I know of, bless his heart. Honestly, it was a shame.

To this day, whenever I think of Waffle House, I think of KK holding that napkin like it was a winning lottery ticket, only to find out the numbers didn't add up. Somewhere out there, that blonde is probably still smiling, and maybe she has more teeth now. Either way, she left a mark. Not just on KK, but on all of us who witnessed the night he shot his shot and learned the hard truth about coffee, confidence, and dental surprises.
