In the movie _The Mighty Ducks_, there's a scene that hit me hard when I first saw it that isn't about kids or skating or slapshots. It's Hans, the old mentor, sitting in his shop late at night, listening to a hockey game on the radio. No screen or highlight reels. Just a voice, and the invisible world of the rink unfolding in his mind. That moment has lived rent-free in my head for decades.

I grew up in the shadow of the University of Georgia, where Saturdays were more than just a day on the calendar. If you live anywhere near the SEC, then you know about football Saturdays. They were the rhythm of life. Back then, we didn't have hundreds of channels or an app that could replay every touchdown. We had maybe six TV stations, and one of them was TBS on Channel 17. And when you couldn't see the game, you listened.

For me, that meant Larry Munson's gravelly urgency painting pictures you could feel in your bones. Later, it was Skip Carey's warm, steady cadence as he called Braves baseball like he was chatting with an old friend over the back fence.

You didn't just hear the plays. You felt the space between them. The crowd murmured like a restless tide. The crack of the bat. The groan of disappointment told you everything you needed to know before the announcer even confirmed it.

When our family's TV gave up the ghost in 1980, Sunday afternoons and evenings became a new kind of ritual. We tuned in to radio teleplays, entire stories acted out in sound, and my imagination got a workout no movie could match. Those shows taught me how sound alone could create whole worlds, and that training carried right over into listening to sports.

That habit, that love, never really left me.

As I got older, my sports tastes grew beyond my Georgia roots. I fell in love with hockey, organically, in the state of Alaska, where winter is more of a constant than a season. The Pittsburgh Penguins caught my ear thanks to Mike Lange, whose calls weren't just accurate, they were art. "Scratch my back with a hacksaw." "Buy Sam a drink and get his dog one, too." I have only been to Pittsburgh once in my life, but Lange made the city feel like an old friend I had known for years.

Even now, when I could stream every game in high definition, I find myself reaching for the radio feed. These days, I follow the Mariners and the Texas Rangers that way, letting the words carry me instead of the pixels.

And hockey? Most of my NHL season plays out in the theater of my mind, because on the radio, every player is faster, every shot is sharper, and every save is just a little more dramatic.

Radio sports are not about convenience anymore. In fact, they are almost the opposite. You have to be still enough to listen, patient enough to let the picture form, and willing to surrender to the storyteller's pace. You cannot scroll through your phone or skip ahead. You cannot watch the instant replay until the mystery is gone.

And yet, there is nothing like it.

I think part of the reason is distance. I have been away from home for so long that the games I grew up with have become lifelines. My Michigan State Spartans, every touchdown, every tipoff, every slapshot, reach me in Texas through radio waves, not video feeds. I can close my eyes and be back in East Lansing in a second, hearing the band, the roar, the hometown joy or heartbreak.

It is the closest thing I have to teleportation.

Some people will never understand it. They will see the grainy signal, the lack of visuals, and think it is missing something. But to me, that missing piece is the gift. It is the space where your mind fills in the gaps, where you get to decide how the sun hits the pitcher's mound, how the puck skitters across the blue line, how the quarterback's breath curls in the cold.

Screens show you what happened. Radio invites you to help make it happen.
