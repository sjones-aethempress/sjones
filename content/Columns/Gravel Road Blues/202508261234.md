They say you never forget your first love, but trust me, your first job will stick with you even longer, and not in a good way. Love fades. That first job lingers like the smell of mop water and broken dreams.

Everyone has one, and everyone swears theirs was the worst. The first job is where you learn to work hard, be humble, and wonder why the mop water always smells worse than it looks. It is a universal rite of passage, like acne or driver's ed, only with less dignity and more name tags.

My first job was at a Winn-Dixie grocery store, back when they were the pride of Southern suburbs and the only place you could buy bologna, motor oil, and a bucket of fried chicken in the same trip. As an entry-level clerk, I was the lowest rung on the ladder, and every day was a comedy of errors with a touch of tragedy.

Walking into my first shift, I was armed with optimism, a freshly ironed shirt, and absolutely no idea what I was doing. I met my boss, who was the walking definition of tough love, except without the love. He greeted me with a grunt and handed me a mop. No tour, no orientation video, just a mop and a bucket that looked like it had seen action in the Spanish-American War.

The learning curve was brutal. The cash register had more buttons than the cockpit of a small plane. Customers barked demands like drill sergeants. And on my second day, I managed to spill hot coffee on a man who swore he was going to sue, but thankfully, he settled for a free box of MoonPies and a pack of Camels.

The real joy of a first job is the tasks you will never forget, mostly because no one else will do them. If you have never scraped gum off the underside of a table with a butter knife, have you even lived? At Winn-Dixie, I learned that humanity is cruel, and nowhere is it crueler than in the frozen food aisle, where people abandon melting ice cream when they change their minds.

Customer service was its own nightmare. I once had a woman return a half-eaten rotisserie chicken because, in her words, it "tasted tired." Another customer demanded extra ice in her soda, but not too much, and gave me the stink eye like I was personally responsible for the global ice shortage. Then there was the eternal comedy of broken machines. The soda fountain ran out of carbonation at least once a week, the mop bucket leaked, and the time clock had to be kicked three times before it would register a punch.

Still, first jobs have a way of teaching you lessons whether you want them or not. Humility is the big one. Nothing puts you in your place like being yelled at by a toddler who insists his lollipop is broken. Teamwork is another lesson, though in reality, it was more about learning who not to trust. There is always one coworker who vanishes every time a truckload of canned goods needs to be stocked. And then there is resilience. If you can survive a shift at a Winn-Dixie on double-coupon day, you can survive just about anything, except maybe speaking in public.

Looking back, it is all hilarious now. At the time, I thought my life was ruined every time a customer cursed at me, but now it makes for a great story. Misery loves company, and that is why first job stories are the ultimate icebreaker. Bring it up at a reunion and watch the room explode into laughter. Everyone has one, whether it is wearing a ridiculous uniform at a fast-food joint or dealing with breakroom coffee that tastes like axle grease.

For all the sweat and indignity, there were silver linings. I was learning time management, conflict resolution, and the fine art of pretending to care while a customer debated over two brands of ketchup. I gained a new appreciation for the value of work and vowed never to complain about a paycheck again, though I admit that promise did not last long. Most importantly, I walked away with a treasure trove of stories that get funnier with time, especially the one about chasing a runaway shopping cart into the plate-glass window of a bank.

Your first job may not be glamorous, but it is the foundation for everything that comes after. It teaches you to endure, to laugh, and to keep a spare shirt in your car. Sure, it is terrible, but it is unforgettable. And is that not what makes life interesting?

So here is to the first jobs: the sweat, the tears, and the grease stains that never come out of your favorite shirt. You survived, and that is worth celebrating, preferably after a long shower and a cold drink.
