When you grow up in Georgia, you don't think of sweet tea as a drink. It is oxygen in liquid form. It's poured at every meal, every family gathering, and every church social worth attending. The recipe isn't written down because everyone knows it by heart: one part tea, four parts sugar, and the vague hope that your dentist never finds out.

My grandmother's kitchen is where I first learned how seriously Georgians take their tea. She brewed it so sweet you half expected it to crystallize before you could pour it over ice. "The secret," she told me once, "is you don't measure sugar with a spoon. You measure it with love." Judging by the consistency of molasses, she must have loved us a whole lot.

I meant the sugar, not the actual tea. With some folks these days, you have to come right out and say it.

So when I moved to Texas, I thought I was safe. It's still the South, right? They say "y'all," fry everything twice, and know their way around a pecan pie. Surely the sweet tea would be the same.

Wrong.

In Texas, you can order "sweet" tea and wind up with a glass that tastes like someone waved a sugar packet near it for moral support. My first sip left me staring at the cup in betrayal, wondering if I had been served decaf by mistake. That was the day I realized I needed a survival plan.

At first, I started pocketing sugar packets from diners and gas stations. They became my little emergency stash, folded into my wallet next to credit cards and old receipts. The first time I pulled one out at a restaurant, I felt like a man smuggling contraband. Ripping open the packet and stirring my tea with all the subtlety of a chemist, I noticed the waiter watching me with the same suspicion reserved for people who talk to pigeons.

Over time, sugar gave way to Stevia, mostly because old sugar packets have a half-life. Let one sit in your wallet for two years, and it starts to look less like sugar and more like a confetti bomb. Stevia was neater, lighter, and it didn't make my jeans look like I was carrying around the ashes of a campfire.

Of course, you still have to deploy it carefully. Picture me at a crowded diner in Tyler, tearing open a packet and stirring like I'm making moonshine in broad daylight. Texans look over at me like I'm trying to season my drink with something the DEA would want to know about.

And heaven help me on the days I forget my packet. Drinking unsweetened tea feels like eating cornbread without butter or biscuits without gravy. Technically possible, yes. Spiritually crushing, also yes.

But here's the thing. Carrying that little packet of Stevia isn't just about fixing a glass of tea. It's about carrying a piece of my youth with me. Sweet tea is more than sugar water. It's tradition, it's hospitality, it's home. When I stir my emergency packet into some pale Texas brew, I am not just sweetening tea. I am calling back porches, summers, and Sunday dinners with people who measured sugar by love.

Every once in a while, I stumble into a Texas café that gets it right. The tea is dark, the sugar is unapologetic, and I swear the first sip could make you forget you were ever homesick. On those days, I can leave the Stevia packet in my wallet and just enjoy the surprise of finding a little Southern solace in the wild.

Still, I carry my stash. Because you never know when the next betrayal will arrive in a glass of iced tea that tastes like sadness. Some people keep a lucky penny in their pocket. Others carry a rabbit's foot or a rosary. Me? I keep a packet of Stevia. It may not ward off bad luck, but it sure keeps bad tea from winning.

Sweet tea may have followed me here from Georgia, but Texas is where I'll keep drinking it my way.
