Somewhere in every office, there is a person who decides to commit an act so bold, so reckless, that it will be remembered for years. They do it without shame, without hesitation, and often without any awareness of the chaos they're about to unleash. I'm talking about microwaving fish in the breakroom.

If you've never experienced this, count yourself among the blessed. It starts as a faint whiff drifting down the hallway, easy to ignore at first. Then it grows. Soon it's not just a smell, it's an event. People pause mid-sentence, tilt their heads, and squint like they've just heard a far-off explosion. Someone mutters, "Oh no." The HVAC system gets involved, circulating the fumes with the efficiency of a gossip chain. Within minutes, the entire office smells like a bait shop in August.

Fish, in general, is not subtle. But fish that's been trapped in a plastic container, sealed overnight, and then blasted in a communal microwave has a special power. It's the olfactory equivalent of a foghorn. You don't just smell it, you feel it. It seeps into your clothes, clings to your hair, and lingers in your nostrils like a bad memory. Three days later, the copier still smells like tilapia, and the potted plant in the corner has given up on life.

The reaction is always the same. A low-grade panic begins to ripple across the cubicles. People start sniffing the air and whispering like they're in a spy thriller. "Do you know who it was?" "Was it Dave?" "No, Dave's out today. Maybe Janice?" Emails start circulating under the subject line "Friendly Reminder About Respecting Shared Spaces." A scented candle appears in the breakroom, which doesn't fix the problem so much as create a new one: now it smells like vanilla-covered haddock.

Every office has different types of fish microwavers. There's the repeat offender, who knows exactly what they're doing but sees it as an act of rebellion. Maybe they are the playgound bullyyou knew as a kid. You have the oblivious one, who genuinely has no idea it's a problem. They're the same person who clips their nails at their desk and doesn't think twice. There's the fitness fanatic who swears fish is the cleanest protein and that "it's not about you, it's about my macros." And finally, there's the tragic one-timer, who makes the mistake once and is forever branded "the fish person" in company lore. Years later, new hires will hear the story with a mixture of awe and revulsion.

If you've ever been in the office when this happens, you know there's a timeline you can draw on a whiteboard in your sleep. It starts with the sound of the microwave beeping. Someone opens the door, and a cloud of steam wafts out like the opening shot of a disaster movie. The offender sits down, fork in hand, completely unaware that they've just triggered a sensory fire alarm. Within an hour, the whispers have turned into open hostility.

Sticky notes in all colors of the rainbow begin to appear on the microwave: "Please Do Not Microwave Fish." Then another note. Then another. Soon the machine is covered in passive-aggressive paper like it's been vandalized by librarians.

Why do people do it? Some are just in a hurry. They brought leftover trout for lunch and never thought twice about it. Others are making a power move, consciously or not. It's hard to ignore someone who can make the entire office smell like low tide. A few might genuinely not notice the smell, which is perhaps the most troubling group of all.

And in some cases, it's a cultural norm. In parts of the world, fish is an everyday lunch that doesn't carry the same stigma. But offices are melting pots, and not all ingredients blend smoothly.

The aftermath of a fish incident can last far longer than the smell itself. Trust in the communal microwave is destroyed. Even reheating coffee feels risky. People start bringing cold lunches or fleeing the building entirely to eat elsewhere. In extreme cases, the microwave becomes a museum piece, unplugged and left in the corner as a warning to future generations.

I know this because I've lived through it. I've been in offices where the Great Salmon Debacle of 2015 was spoken of in hushed tones. I've watched otherwise reasonable adults form alliances over their shared trauma. I've even seen a "microwave permit" policy introduced, which required people to sign a log before heating anything more potent than soup.

And here's the thing: it doesn't have to be this way. There are foods we all agree should not go into a shared microwave. Fish is at the top of the list. Boiled eggs are right behind it. Broccoli rounds out the top three. If your lunch has the potential to make the conference room smell like a crime scene, maybe that's a sign it's time for Plan B.

Microwaving fish in the breakroom may seem like a small act, but it's not. It changes the energy of the office. It inspires legends. It shapes reputations. And once you've been marked as the fish person, you never really shake it. So if you're standing there at 12:15, staring at your container of last night's salmon, remember this: you are holding the power to alter the course of workplace history.

Use it wisely.
