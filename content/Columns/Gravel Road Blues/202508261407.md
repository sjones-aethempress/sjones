It's a strange feeling when you're in the frozen food section picking out tater tots, and suddenly you hear your high school prom song. Is that Aerosmith's "Janie's Got A Gun"? In a HEB? Where exactly is Janie _now_? I nearly dropped a carton of milk once when Pearl Jam came over the speakers.

Pearl Jam, y'all.

The same band I played loud enough to rattle my parents' windows was now helping someone decide between frozen peas and green beans.

That was the day I realized grocery store Muzak was not random. It is part of a grand conspiracy to make us feel old enough to stockpile prune juice and arthritis cream.

Back in the 60s and 70s, Muzak was safe, bland, and forgettable. It was all flutes, harps, and violins. You could pick out canned peaches without suddenly remembering your first heartbreak. The goal was to be pleasant but invisible, like wallpaper with a pulse.

Or any government official you don't actually need.

It was soothing in its way. Nobody ever stood in the cereal aisle and cried because the pan flute solo reminded them of their senior year. Muzak back then kept its distance.

Then came some marketing genius who figured out people buy more bread when they hear Bread. Suddenly, the playlists shifted from faceless background noise to actual songs people recognized. It worked too. Nostalgia sells.

Instead of violins, you got "Close to You" by the Carpenters while choosing which brand of detergent might get the barbecue stains out. It felt almost charming. Almost.

The trouble is that nostalgia keeps moving. What once was meant for Baby Boomers has shifted to Gen Xers and Millennials. One day it was the Carpenters. The next one was Nirvana. Lord help us all if they start playing Green Day in the yogurt section.

There is actual science behind this. Psychologists say familiar songs trigger dopamine and warm memories, which make us more likely to spend. A well-timed Elton John chorus can apparently push you to buy cookies you swore you did not need.

Think of it as Pavlov's dogs with guitars. Instead of salivating, we start tossing things into the cart because Journey told us not to stop believing.

I personally do not view myself as a quitter.

And who is making these playlists anyway? Somewhere out there is a grocery store DJ, a 50-year-old in a headset grinning while they cue up "Everybody Hurts" just as you reach for a rotisserie chicken.

The hard truth is this: if you are hearing the songs of your youth at the grocery store, you are the target. The stores know who is pushing carts now. If you have a mortgage, a minivan, and a thing for 90s alt-rock, congratulations. You’re who they’re after.

It is unsettling when your favorite songs turn into background noise. Hearing The Eagles while shopping is one thing. Hearing Rage Against the Machine while comparing cereal prices is another. Nothing screams midlife crisis like nodding along to "Killing in the Name" while holding a box of Cheerios.

Sometimes I think this is all intentional. They play "Don't Stop Believin'" to keep us from rioting in the bread aisle when eggs cost eight dollars. They slip in Fleetwood Mac to soften the blow of the credit card bill. It is psychological warfare, Southern edition.

And the worst part? It works. I will be humming along to Tom Petty, dropping an extra pint of ice cream into the cart, and not realizing until I get home that nostalgia just robbed me blind.

At some point, you have to give in. If they’re going to play "Sweet Home Alabama," I’m going to sing along, even if my kids abandon me in the snack aisle out of embarrassment. Don’t get me started on “A Country Boy Can Survive.” Or anything by Bocephus, really. Better yet, let me take over the playlist. Imagine the dairy section rocking to Metallica. Yogurt sales would skyrocket.

Of course, the next generation will get theirs. One day, my kids will be standing in line for milk and bread when a grocery store speaker pipes up with Justin Bieber. And they will cringe, and I will smile. Justice comes for us all.

Pain and Agony deem us all equally worthless.

The truth is, Muzak is not just noise anymore. It is the soundtrack of our aging. A trip to the store can send you down memory lane before you even hit the checkout.

Perhaps getting older isn't so bad if it comes with a good soundtrack. Just keep the fish sticks and the Fleetwood Mac coming.

But I swear, if they start playing Nickelback, I am switching to online grocery delivery. This is how you remind me of what I really am. An AARP card-carrying member.
