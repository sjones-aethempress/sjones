It was a Tuesday at 9:07 a.m. when my phone buzzed with the most terrifying words a person can receive: "We need to talk." No context. No follow-up. Just existential dread in four syllables.

My stomach dropped faster than a Longhorns rival on a bad day, and my mind started replaying every possible thing I had ever done wrong since birth. Did I forget to pay a bill? Did I offend someone at church? Did I accidentally reply-all on an email I should have kept private?

This column is not about the words themselves. It is about the six hours of psychological warfare that follow. The silent torture chamber created by one vague text message.

The first sixty seconds are always the hardest. You try not to faint, drop your phone, or fling it into a nearby body of water. Fight or flight kicks in, but neither option seems viable because you are glued to that screen. Overthinking begins immediately. Did I forget someone's birthday? Did I say something dumb at the family barbecue? Did the IRS finally track me down? A helpful tip for anyone sending this kind of message: if you insist on dropping a bomb, at least include an estimated time of detonation.

By the end of the first hour, denial sets in. You convince yourself it is probably nothing. Maybe they just want to know what's for dinner. Maybe they are excited about the nice weather. Yes, that is definitely it. Just small talk. You scroll back through every conversation, including ones from three years ago about pineapple on pizza, looking for clues. You even call your best friend, who assures you it is nothing, but you still start drafting an apology note in case it is actually something.

Hours two and three are where frustration arrives. Who sends a message like that and then vanishes into thin air? Do they enjoy watching other people squirm? By hour three, I was tempted to text back, "You know what? Never mind. I do not want to talk anymore either." It is like waiting at the dentist's office, except the drill is your own imagination.

Then comes bargaining. Hours four and five are spent making promises to yourself and to the universe. If they just tell me it is not bad news, I will never complain about slow Wi-Fi again. I will even stop stealing my neighbor's Amazon packages. You check your phone every thirty seconds, refreshing until the battery cries for mercy. Even Siri sounds judgmental when you ask for updates. "Calm down, it's probably nothing," she seems to say. But you cannot bring yourself to respond. What if texting back makes it worse? So you sit in emotional limbo, gnawing on your fingernails and whatever is left of your sanity.

By hour six, resignation takes hold. You are no longer panicking. You are numb. You accept that whatever comes next will be your fate. Maybe you will lose a friend, maybe you will lose your job, maybe the entire state of Texas will revoke your residency card. Fine. You will face it with a pint of ice cream and the business card of a therapist. You are ready.

And then the phone buzzes again. You pick it up with trembling hands, heart pounding like you are awaiting a final exam grade. The message reads: "Oh, I just wanted to know if you could feed my cat this weekend."

Six hours of panic. For a cat.

There are a few lessons to be drawn here. First, if you are going to send a "we need to talk" message, go ahead and add the follow-up immediately. Do not let innocent people spiral into six hours of self-imposed suffering. Second, life is far too short for text message mind games. From now on, if anyone tries that stunt on me, I am responding with, "Sure, but only after you Venmo me five dollars for emotional damages."

Because nothing ruins a perfectly good Tuesday faster than four little words followed by six hours of silence.
