The air outside is thicker than Mama's gravy. You step out the door in June, and it feels like someone just wrapped you in a wet quilt fresh out of the dryer. The cicadas are already screaming, the dog has given up on chasing squirrels, and the asphalt looks like it is breathing. Summer in the South is not just a season. It is a battle of survival, a yearly wrestling match with the sun itself.

To cope, Southerners develop what I call Southern Heat Grief. Much like grief, it comes in stages, and you go through every one of them before Labor Day.

The first stage is denial. Every year around late May, we look around and say, "It's not that hot. Just a little warm. I'll be fine." We lie to ourselves. We tell anyone who will listen that sweet tea and a ceiling fan can fix everything. We even walk around in jeans or, heaven help us, flannel shirts in June because we are still clinging to the idea that it is technically spring.

I have seen grown men mowing their lawns in denim because they refuse to admit defeat. Their faces are red, their shirts are soaked through, but they will not give up. "It's not that hot," they say, right before passing out in the flower bed.

We’ll do just about anything for entertainment.

Denial eventually gives way to pure, unfiltered rage. The sun feels like a personal enemy, trying to fry you like a catfish in peanut oil. The humidity sits on your chest like an insult, daring you to complain. The neighbors' lawnmower sounds louder and more offensive in July than it ever did in March.

The air conditioner becomes a source of both love and fury. You want it to work harder, but you are also terrified to open the electric bill. Families in the South have fought wars over thermostat settings. I have seen perfectly happy households brought to the brink because someone nudged it from 75 to 74. "Are you trying to bankrupt us?" is a question no child should have to answer while sweating on the sofa.

By mid-July, we are all negotiating. We make deals with God. "Lord, if you just let a cold front sweep in, I will go to church every Sunday and tithe with enthusiasm." We plead with the utility companies. "Please, just lower my bill by twenty dollars, and I will name my next child after you." We even try bargaining with the weather itself. "Just one cool evening, just one breeze that doesn't feel like a hair dryer, and I promise to stop complaining."

These bargains, of course, are useless. September arrives, and it’s still hotter than Hades in a frying pan. You look at the forecast, hoping for mercy, and all you see is 97 degrees with a side of despair.

Tears are subject to availability.

Once bargaining fails, the sadness takes over. This is the stage where Southerners melt into their sofas and live off popsicles, sweet tea, and Netflix. You raid the fridge for anything cold, not because you are hungry, but because standing in front of the open door feels like a spa treatment.

You compare yourself to a melting candle or a wilted magnolia. Even the shade looks like it’s given up. Self-pity sets in. "Even the mosquitoes are miserable," you mutter as one buzzes weakly by your ear. And yet, everyone around you is just as miserable. There is a strange comfort in the collective suffering, but that doesn’t make it any cooler.

Finally, sometime in August, acceptance arrives. Southerners stop fighting the heat and start living with it. You pour yourself a glass of lemonade, sit on the porch, and let the sweat happen. You accept that you are going to stick to the church pew on Sunday morning, and you make peace with it.

The coping mechanisms kick in. You joke about frying an egg on the sidewalk. You laugh when the mosquitoes look like they are begging for ice water. You learn that if you are not sweating, you are probably not Southern. You take pride in the fact that you can handle hurricanes, humidity, and relatives all in the same week. The heat becomes less of an enemy and more of an old, annoying friend.

In the end, the Southern summer is a test of character. It will make you cuss, it will make you cry, and it will absolutely make you sweat. But by the time fall arrives, we will brag about surviving another one. We will wear our sweat stains like badges of honor and pretend it was not that bad after all.

Because down here, we don’t measure seasons by the calendar. We measure them by the sweat stains.
