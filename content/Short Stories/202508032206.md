It was once said that 'Coffee, to be good, must be black as night, sweet as love and hot as hell.' Some places attribute that quote to John Milton or Dante. Still, it was actually a Russian quote from the fabled anarchist Mikhail Alexandrovich Bakunin, a fellow who appeared to enjoy good coffee, likely at little to no charge, and long walks throughout the Russian Empire.

Mikhail was Russian, so the Western audience was determined to forget about him. It wasn't suitable for news sales unless something bad could be said about them, and it didn't matter if it was true or not as long as the whole lot was being diminished in some way.

Leo examined himself quickly in the reflection of the glass door at Café Hypnos and determined he was presentable enough to enter. Bakunin likely had his own favorite barista.

That keeps you coming back.

No one seemed to know where this little shop had actually come from. It was an odd situation; one day, there were two stores, back-to-back, and seemingly the next day, this coffee shop sprouted up right between them.

Speaking of sprouting, the lovely flower Leo came to set his eyes on every morning was the barista, Marin. She lived above the shop in a small apartment no one had ever seen the inside of, nor would they ever. But she was every bit as dependable as taxes or death.

He gazed at her as he opened the door, a lovely porcelain automaton positioned in a smooth, flowing glide as she executed orders for the line of customers in front of her; at the climax of the coffee creation, she often had something else to say, and the opinions on this varied.

As for Leo, all she'd ever said to him was small talk and a thanks. But it was enough for him.

Marin appeared to be nodding off again. She had an abnormal proclivity to drowsiness, a state she just couldn't seem to shake. Working in a coffee shop was the most natural thing for her since she was never awake enough to do anything else, and by the time she appeared to return to the land of the living, it was time to close shop.

Her blondish hairdo seemed just as drowsy, stuck in a Scandinavian hell with a bowl-like centerpiece carved from her stunted mane and two little offshoots on each side that dangled and bounced as an afterthought. She looked a lot like an anime character someone had drawn and then moved to a new project. Those little offshoots, swaying in her movements like loose reins...

Leo didn't know if they were for show or steering. The next thought made him blush, but he understood why his mind went there.

The entire diorama of coiffure reminded him of an artist he'd heard of once and liked. She'd been from some country over there, Sweden perhaps. He'd listened to her entire album while assembling a side table one Sunday afternoon. He was a man of directions and industrial-oriented art.

As he waited through Marin's interaction with the customers in front of him, she muddled with the drinks at a speed still unnatural. Her autopilot was better than most other baristas' best efforts, even when fully awake.

She was pretty, in her early twenties, and while he couldn't come close to understanding why she had settled on slinging Joe as a profession, he was immensely grateful Marin had. She kept him more awake than she could keep herself.

On this morning, Marin stopped, looking through the steam as if she were getting a network download. She shifted her gaze to Leo and, with a slight shake of her head, said, "The glass stairs weren’t safe, I don't think."

Leo cocked his head. She was waiting for a response.

Not seeming to get the reaction she was waiting for, Marin shrugged and continued with his coffee. As an afterthought, she offered, "You dreamed of falling, right?"

He was lost. This was not what he had expected at all.

Two nights prior, Leo had dreamed of being on a first date with a beautiful girl that he didn't recognize. For some reason, they were in a house, being playful and flirtatious. Between the second and third floors was a glass staircase from which he could see the hardwood below.

His lovely playmate scampered up the staircase without issue, and Leo enthusiastically followed her. That mistake came to fruition around stair number seven; when the case broke, he fell, flailing, and promptly woke up.

The girl appeared in a random social media profile the next day. She was married. He had no desire to meet the spouse. Talk about dodging bullets.

It was enough that he alone knew of this episode that he tried to hide the fact that he intentionally was trying to look up his date's skirt as she bounded the stairs. That was a natural male reaction, right?

Now, a stranger, or an acquaintance at the very least, knew about this scene? It made the blood run cold. If she could somehow tell him about his dreams, what else could she know about? It felt more like deep-state spy surveillance than mysticism.

"How do you know about that?" Leo asked, trying to remain nonchalant.

"Dreams cling to steam. That's why I brew so strong. You bring them in; I just read them." She replied with a look on her face that seemed to convey momentarily nothing short of enlightenment.

It was a Tuesday that Leo could remember as if it were yesterday. Marin had an unusual moment of clarity and touched her white towel to his arm, an action very out of place for her but the closest she ever came to physical contact. She leaned in and captured his gaze as if her robins-egg baby blue eyes were a tractor beam from a space story.

"You haven't had this one yet. But when you do, don't follow the music."

He tried to blow it off, to ignore the split-second urgency in her eyes, but it lingered with him and resurfaced in random thoughts throughout the week.

Leo asked a few of the other patrons about her track record out of Marin's earshot, and everyone he talked to agreed on one thing: Whatever the deal was with this girl, she was legit. It didn't matter how trivial or how life-changing the phrases uttered by their favorite barista were, but more important was they had always been right.

A fellow named Barry, who he knew was an actuary at an insurance firm in the Downtown area, told Leo about his interaction. He'd had a dream where he found himself in a multiple-car pileup on the Interstate. In the dream, his legs were missing from the accident.

He further explained the terror and panic of being in the accident and how he felt so helpless and alone.

"I came in the next mornin', and ole Marin just looked at me and said, 'Darlin', we're gonna stay on surface streets today, okay?' What was I gonna do? I know better. I stayed on the frontage road."

"How did that work out?" Leo asked.

"I found my pileup just past National. Exactly where I would have been if I'd taken the freeway. You just don't question her. You listen."

Leo tried discussing the whole thing with Marin on several occasions, but there were only a few minutes to have the interaction, and she wasn't willing to disrupt the line of customers long enough to give him a lecture on it.

"The dreams are like little travelers," she tried to explain, "They come in on steam and float through it like water through a cloth. I can't control what they are or aren't. I just see them and tell what I see."

There was little to nothing to explain further, and Leo just decided to continue and see what happened, if anything.

Monday night would bring the scene Leo was waiting for with a grimace. He could faintly hear the music, the thumping, the overactive sonic heartbeat that increased in volume as he swung open a metal door that had experienced life with battering rams at some point in its existence.

The scene before him was an abandoned underground rail station and a rave of some sort unfolding as if it were a video game. While you could see and sense the mildew and decay of the floor and walls, thankfully, you couldn't smell it.

The tune was catchy if nothing else.

Looking into the station was a pivot point of sorts, that feeling you get when you're not sure what you're waiting on: a train that may never arrive or the continuance of life itself.

Leo watched as the dancing throng bounced in rhythm like a lot of Mardi Gras-colored Mexican Jumping Beans on a sieve. It was calming in a way, just like those mathematical fractal screensavers that create new and random designs with full color. The dancers themselves were masked in face coverings stuck somewhere between Fat Tuesday and the film _Eyes Wide Shut._

Masks serve a useful, even if unintended, service in making sure that the outside viewer looks deeply into the wearer's eyes because there is really nothing else of substance left to inspect.

A lady in a frenzied-looking cat mask sluggishly pointed towards a door on the back wall that had also seen better days. It resembled the entrance to a boiler closet, the dirty brown door frame proudly displaying abuse.

Two others, also wearing equally disturbing masks, showed toothy grins and motioned to the door as well. They were all seemingly in on a cosmic joke that had escaped Leo.

The door held a simple sign that was hand-lettered, almost scrawled on a piece of copy paper and brusquely taped to the peeling door that read simply,

ALL Answers Here

He found it quite odd and chilling, especially since it resembled Comic Sans. Who in their right mind uses that font?

The beat on the other side of the door was double the speed of the one beside him, and it was dizzying. He couldn't imagine what it would be like once he opened the door.

Leo saw the reflection of his palm on the doorknob, and a feeling of panic washed over him. Something was horribly wrong about all of this. The dancers had all shifted in their gyrations to where they faced him, and that was just plain creepy.

The dancer closest to him flashed a teasing grin and motioned at the door. She made him realize how much context the mask removed. His imagination filled in the details, almost to the point of opening the door in reaction.

His panic returned, and it was not a feeling of novel fear but more of a final, end-of-life-type terror. Leo saw a flash with blue eyes that made his heart skip a beat, and they were telling him once again not to follow the music.

Marin.

"Yeah, I don't think this is my party," Leo said, backing his way out of the room. The group continued their frivolity as they maintained a predatory eye on him as he made his way out.

He woke up in a pool of sweat and a mouth as dry as a desert. He blinked, confirmed his existence, and had a taste for coffee.

It was Tuesday morning.

Marin stopped in her motion and looked up at Leo. With a pause, she gave a quick nod of acknowledgment and confirmed, "Good. You would’ve lost something important."

He wanted to know exactly _what_ he would have lost, but it was clear that the conversation was over. That was just the way the steam worked, and the gift was temporary.

A few days later, Leo was thumbing the edge of a new journal he'd purchased to record what he could remember of his dreams. It was a soft-cover Moleskine, the kind he usually found too precious to write in. Still, something about Marin's sleepy-eyed certainty made the expense feel ceremonial, like dream mapping required better materials.

He'd begun waking at odd hours, compelled to scribble down whatever fragments he could capture before they disintegrated. The entries were uneven at first: a tooth floating in a bowl of oranges, a subway tunnel that roared like an animal, his mother saying, _"You'll miss your chance if you blink too slow."_ But soon, the habit thickened into obsession. He'd started carrying the journal with him, flipping through pages between meetings, in grocery store lines, while brushing his teeth. Sleep became less about rest and more about content acquisition.

Still, the dreams were slippery. They changed when he wasn't looking. Some pages seemed to rewrite themselves; for instance, he'd go back and find that a hallway he remembered as narrow was now infinite or a woman with wings had become a man with smoke for eyes. Leo began wondering: _If we can know dreams ahead of time... are we supposed to change them?_ Or were they warnings? Promises? Maps to a place we weren't supposed to reach?

At the café, Marin had grown more cryptic.

"If you open the drawer, you'll forget your name," she told a man in a charcoal suit, handing him his espresso like it was a ticket to a play he hadn't agreed to attend.

Another morning, she leaned over the counter toward a teenager in earbuds and whispered, "Don't answer the call from the ocean." She said it gently as if she were asking him to wear a scarf in winter. He blinked at her, half-grin, half-confused, and walked away with his caramel latte sloshing slightly.

Some people laughed. Others gave her a wide berth, unsure whether she was cursed, gifted, or just extremely online.

But then the artist came.

He was new to town or passing through. Leo hadn't seen him before. Wore layered linen like he expected wind indoors. He listened carefully as Marin gave her usual half-lidded warning—this time, a soft: "If you paint the birds with teeth, you won't come back." He smiled that amused, patronizing smile artists often reserve for metaphors.

He came back three days in a row. Sketched in a leather notebook. Ordered only black coffee. On the fourth day, he didn't show.

Two weeks later, his rental apartment was emptied. The neighbor said he'd paid through the end of the month and vanished. Left behind a single painting on the wall, it was something the landlord couldn't move or cover without chills crawling up her neck.

Leo saw it once before the gallery took it.

A canvas split into two halves: sky and sea, but wrong somehow. The sky was too dark and the ocean too knowing. The birds, dozens of them, hovered in mid-dive, beaks jagged and serrated, all pointed at a tiny red figure swimming for shore.

Leo stared for a long time. He felt like he was remembering something from a dream he hadn't had yet.

Back at the café, Marin wouldn't talk about the painting. She wouldn't say anything, really, except to Leo.

To him, she said:

"Some dreams aren't warnings. They're instructions."

Leo started writing faster after that. He didn't know what he was trying to change, stop, or become, but he knew the journal wasn't for remembering anymore.

It was for _building something._ Something that hadn't happened yet.

He needed better answers, and that led to a slightly longer conversation, one of the longest they ever had.

"What I don't get is if you can see the dreams, why don't you do more with them? It seems almost like a gift to me," he asked as she began her brewing.

"It doesn't work that way," Marin said, not so much with a sense of exasperation but more of resignation. It was clear that she'd had this question before and had spent even more time thinking about it. "I only see what people are already carrying. I can't change the dream. Just brew around it," she continued, "It's a bit messy, but I guess it comes with the territory."

She paused for a moment, her hands resting lightly on the counter like she had forgotten they were supposed to move. There was something quieter about her today like she was listening for a sound far away or waiting for a word she could almost remember.

"I haven't had my own dream since I started this job," she said finally. Her voice was soft, almost apologetic. "Not a real one. Just flashes. Static."

Leo frowned. "How long?"

She gave a small, tired smile. It didn't reach her eyes. "Since I was sixteen," she said. "I'm twenty-three now, so... seven years of reruns and guest stars."

There was no bitterness, only something that sounded like wear. Like someone describing a house they used to live in before the roof started leaking and the windows swelled shut.

"I think that's the trade," she said. "I get to see what's coming for everyone else, but I don't get to go anywhere myself. No stories of my own. No wild symbols or strange places to wake up from. Just this." She motioned around her. The espresso machine hissed behind her. A syrup bottle tipped over and clinked against the counter. Rain tapped the window like it wanted something.

She blinked slowly, the way people do when they're trying not to cry or trying not to fall asleep.

"It's harder to filter than it used to be. Like trying to hold on to fog."

"Sounds almost like you need a new job," Leo interjected gently, trying to exhibit some compassion.

"Well, you can't ever have just one job, can you? There's always something else you hafta do after the thing you're supposed to be doing. For me, it's coffee and dreams. The magic's found in the in-between spaces: steam, sleep, memory, love. Never what you thought you were really looking at."

She rubbed her eyes again as she began to lid his drink and appeared to have forgotten something.

"Leo?" she asked more as a confirmation, "That's a lion." Grabbing her pen, she began to doodle again. "I'll give you a kitty, then. Big kitties are cute."

Sure enough, he saw the cartoon cat face with a makeshift mane that appeared to be closer to a hairy, smiling doughnut with a triangular nose than a large feline, but in Leo's mind, it was the thought that counted.

He was there for the coffee and impromptu advice from Marin, not a free sketch from Da Vinci.

The next few visits blurred together, a quiet rhythm of caffeine, cryptic warnings, and half-finished journal entries. Nothing out of place. Nothing urgent.

Marin looked different that morning. She had always moved with a sort of dreamy calm as if she were half-swimming through the day, but now she was sharp around the edges. Pale. Staring too long at nothing in particular.

Leo had just opened his mouth to ask if she was alright when she cut him off. Her voice was steady but hushed as if she didn't want the walls to hear.

"Tonight's dream will try to convince you it's reality," she said. "But it isn't. Don't believe it. If you do, you won't wake up."

Leo blinked. He almost smiled, waiting for her to tag on something cryptic and whimsical like she usually did. But nothing came. Marin just looked at him, serious and scared. She reached out with an actual, deliberate gesture and touched his arm.

Her fingers were hot like fire. Leo stiffened as her touch made him come to life in a way he hadn't expected. Marin never touched people. Not on purpose.

"Marin?"

She pulled her hand back like it had cost her something.

"I can't explain it," she said. "I tried. It's not safe."

She turned away from him, opened a drawer under the counter, and pulled out an envelope. It was heavy cardstock, the kind used for wedding invitations or warnings. She ripped receipt paper from the feed, scribbled something on it, and slipped it into the envelope. With a quick lick on the seal and a slap, she slid it across the counter without looking at him.

"Open it only if you forget me."

Then she walked off to help the next customer like she hadn't just dropped a match onto something flammable. Her voice shifted into her usual rhythm, offering strange flavor notes and impossible advice to a man ordering a vanilla latte. Leo stood there a little longer than he should have, the envelope warm in his palm, like it knew it was on a timer.

He didn't open it. Not yet. But he couldn't stop thinking about it.

And then, the thinking stopped.

Leo opened his eyes to soft morning light filtering through sheer curtains. The skyline outside his window was unfamiliar but beautiful—glass towers and distant cranes, an orderly hum in the distance. He was lying in a bed with linen so fine it made him second-guess the weight of his own limbs.

The space was too clean, too curated. Muted artwork. Coffee table books with gold-lettered spines. A faint smell of citrus and leather. Somewhere across the apartment, someone was humming.

He sat up. This wasn't his apartment. At least, he didn't think so. Then again, something about it felt oddly right.

"Hey, sleepyhead," said a warm voice.

A woman walked in, smiling like she'd done it a thousand times before. She wore a silk robe and had a towel wrapped around her hair. Her features were gentle, symmetrical, and slightly unreal—like the memory of a dream before it slips away.

"Rissa?" he asked, not sure where the name had come from.

She grinned. "Finally awake?"

He blinked. "I… think so."

"You had one of those dreams again, didn't you? The café one."

He opened his mouth but said nothing.

"You always come out of it a little foggy. But you're here. This is real," she said, pressing a kiss to his forehead. "You've always lived here. You're just groggy."

He let Clarissa's words settle into his brain. The more he looked around, the more everything clicked. The books on the shelf had his name on them. Bestsellers. There was a manuscript in progress on the desk, pages clipped neatly as if waiting for him to return. For some reason, he always seemed to name his books with some animal in the title. A coffee cup rested beside a half-finished painting that looked like a landscape he'd never seen and yet somehow missed.

Leo knew the other painting; it was the one they called the "creepy art" because it had two halves of sky and sea, but it set the nerves on edge. The sky dark and the ocean too knowing. It appeared to be done in a fit of depression. The birds, dozens of them, hovered in mid-dive, beaks jagged and serrated, all pointed at a tiny red figure swimming for shore.

They imagined the tiny figure was the artist because he'd painted this and was never seen again. Since the body was never found, it was still a cold case, but the general assumption was suicide.

Leo had heard the story at a gallery and purchased it because, for some reason, it was hauntingly familiar, as if he'd known the artist from somewhere, even though he knew he didn't.

Everything was perfect.

Until he went to change clothes.

He adjusted his pillow and felt something slide beneath the fabric. Reaching under, he pulled out the envelope.

It was already open.

Inside was a single coffee receipt, folded once. On the back, in handwriting he somehow recognized:

**This isn't your life. Wake up. Come back. We still need you.**

That single sentence cracked everything open. Suddenly, the penthouse felt like a set. Marin bloomed in his memory—not her name at first, but her smell, her laugh, the way her hair changed with the weather.

The scent of coffee. The sting of her touch. The joke about lions.

So, if this wasn't the right life, then where was he? And how did he get back to where he was supposed to be?

He turned around. Rissa was there, smiling again, but something had shifted. She looked too still. Too complete. She was the kind of person dreams build when they want you to stay asleep.

Leo stared at her, realizing he had no idea who she was.

His chest tightened. For the first time since waking up, he felt afraid. Not because the life was bad, but because it was too perfect.

Too safe.

Too easy to believe.

And now, he wasn't sure how much time he had left to escape it.

Sometimes, the subtlety of the waking moment comes in with the grace of a cast iron skillet to the face. Leo winced and blinked.

_Great. What's behind door number three this time?_

He surveyed the room. Gone were the expensive sheets, just the box store version that had suited him well so far. He wasn't sure why he had a taste for all the finer things, but apparently, they were there.

On a nearby chair were his usual khaki shorts, suitable for almost any time of year, as well as one of his Hawaiian shirts, which was Leo's everyday attire. They were more shirt than they were Hawaii, but the baggy cut suited him.

He had no jewelry. On the shelf above his desk was his solitary novel, _The Exciting Life of Cats._ He was proud of it, even if it hadn't sold anything of note, and most folks didn't realize it was a mystery book until they read it. They were stolen more than purchased. He was somewhat okay with that. He lost more copies inside Hypnos than he ever sold outright.

_The Café!_

Leo's urgency to get dressed removed most of the tactile capability in his hands. They were shaking and just didn't want to work. Sucking in a deep breath, he tried to focus and steady himself. The first flash in his mind was the face of Marin, intent on her work and the steam, always finding a way to inject a genuine smile into the morning.

"Everybody needs at least one good thing in their day. If it's just a smile from me to make their day better, it's free for me and good for them," she had told him one morning in between providing caffeination and contemplation.

After the rush of getting to the coffee shop like he was bustling a child to the Emergency Room, Leo saw a sight that he hadn't expected and didn't want to see. The café sat dormant, its windows boarded up with thin plywood and a sign that no longer held power.

To his right, a man of his age sat in a panel truck, flipping through some paperwork and consulting a handheld device. He was preparing for the day's deliveries and noticed Leo observing him. The logo on his work polo flashed as the man turned to face him.

The delivery man gestured and said, "They closed down a few weeks ago, I think. Weird place. Good coffee. It was cool, just weird. I liked it, myself." He fiddled with the loose papers in his hand and offered offhand, "The barista was easy on the eyes, too," with a shrug. It was as if he wanted to make a case for why it should be remembered.

"A few weeks?" Leo confirmed.

"Yeah," he stopped to calculate, "I say about two weeks. The last time I was in there, I'd just got paid, and I get paid again tomorrow."

Leo nodded, and he felt the disturbance in the air before he saw it.

Perched on the front step of the café was a cup with his name on it. It was a good day to have a short name, as his was written in large letters so it wouldn't be missed. It had the scribbled heart and the simple lion with a mane doodle. Steam was still escaping in a tendril flowing to the sky from the sipping hole in the lid.

He walked over warily and confirmed that the cup, in fact, had his name on it. It was still hot. His brain told him the best course of action. And as usual, he ignored it and took a sip.

This was his order.

Leo lifted the cup towards the deliveryman in a salute and said, "They got it right." As he left, the man watched him in complete confusion.

"I'll be damned," he stated, as if anyone was listening.

Twelve days later, the boards were gone, and a light once again shone in the café, but the sign was different. The hues were softer and more of a blue pastel. Café Somnus, it now read.

This shop felt different, even from the outside. As he moved in on the door, he realized that the girl he was looking at wasn't Marin.

She was a few years younger, had jet-black hair, and a Joan Jett confidence that was more exciting than the look on her face. For some reason, this place always seemed to bring the sleepiness out of anyone who dared stand behind the counter. Leo took a moment to drink her in. The girl was pretty in a way that made it feel like cosmic law.

Her emerald green eyes locked into him as she announced, "I'm Astrid. I live upstairs. Prices are the same, everything's the same. Kinda. The same , but not the same, if you get my meaning." Astrid grabbed a paper cup and set to work.

Leo recognized that he'd not given her his order.

Looking up, she said, "You're the one who held on, huh? She told me you'd come."

"Where did Marin go?" Leo asked cautiously.

"I don't really know. But I bet wherever she is, she's asleep. I know I would be. I'm already tired, and I just got down here. Need coffee myself."

Astrid finished with the drink and secured the lid to it. She was drawing on the side and writing his name on it as she expressed, "Marin said something to me about how knowing the future is meaningless without the courage to face it. I think it was more for her than for me. But now I'm here, so there's that."

It had nothing to do with her or with Marin. That was a message intended solely for him.

She handed the drink to him with a wink, perhaps a bit more seductively than she'd intended. It was the moment of truth, and Leo took a sip.

Double shot mocha, heavy cream to fill.

Leo smiled and, with a gracious nod, confirmed, "Perfect. I'll see you tomorrow."

Sometimes, we wake up not because the dream has ended but because someone has pulled us gently home.

Leo decided that the next book would have to be titled something involving puppies. It was just time to go write it and find out.
